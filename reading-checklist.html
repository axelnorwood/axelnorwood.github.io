Reading Checklist

Track your reading habits, strategies, and progress. Monitor how you grow as a reader with simple tools designed for reflection and improvement.

Getting Started

Before you read, prepare your mind to notice important things.

Knowing your reason helps you focus on what matters. Different types of reading need different attention.

  • To be persuaded: Look for opinions and arguments. What is the author trying to change your mind about?
  • To be informed: Look for facts and explanations. What does the author want you to know?
  • To be entertained: Look for characters, feelings, and story. How do you feel as you read?
  • Multiple reasons: Many texts have more than one purpose. Which feels strongest?

Thinking about what you already know gives your brain a head start and helps you connect new ideas.

  • Think of 2-3 things you already know about this topic or story
  • Look at the title or cover — what do you expect?
  • Does this remind you of something? A film, a book, something in real life?
  • Pick one question to keep in mind while you read

While You Read

These strategies help you understand and remember what you're reading.

Good reading sounds smooth and natural — not choppy or word-by-word.

  • Read in groups of words (3-4 words together) instead of one at a time
  • Use punctuation: full stop = pause; comma = small pause; exclamation = energy
  • Read the same bit 2-3 times. It always sounds better by the third time
Pro tip: If you keep getting words wrong, start the whole sentence again from the beginning.

Not every word matters equally. Learn which ones to focus on.

  • Tricky words: Mark unfamiliar words and try to figure out meaning from context
  • Connector words: "However", "because", "although" — these link ideas together
  • Repeated words: If the author uses a word again and again, it's important
  • Surprising word choices: Why did the author pick that exact word?
When stuck: Read around the tricky word, look for explanations nearby, or break the word into smaller parts.

When something doesn't make sense, try these approaches.

  • I don't understand the meaning: Go back to the beginning of the sentence. Read the sentences before and after.
  • I can't say the word: Break it into chunks. Try different vowel sounds. Look for small words hiding inside.
  • Picture it: Close your eyes and imagine what's happening. If you can't picture it, you haven't understood it yet.
  • Look for connecting words: "Then", "because", "but", "so" show how ideas link

After You Read

Reflect on what you've read to make sure it really stuck.

Strong readers use these six strategies to check their understanding:

  • Vocabulary: What does this word mean here? Why did the author choose it?
  • Inference: What is the text hinting at but not saying? Read between the lines.
  • Prediction: What do you think will happen next? What in the text made you think that?
  • Explain: How is the author using language? Find something interesting and explain the effect.
  • Retrieve: Find and copy facts directly from the text — exactly as it says.
  • Sequence: Put the main events in order, or sum up what happened in your own words.

Before you move on, ask yourself these questions:

  • Can you sum up this section in one sentence?
  • Do you know who, what, where, when, and why?
  • Was anything different from what you expected? Why?
  • Does this connect to something in your life or another book?
Remember: If you can't answer these questions, go back and re-read the section.

Track Your Progress

Use these prompts to monitor your growth as a reader.

Reflect on your reading regularly to see how you're improving:

  • What strategy helped me most this week?
  • Which type of reading (fiction, non-fiction) do I enjoy more?
  • What was difficult this week? What did I do about it?
  • How has my reading changed since last month?
  • What's one thing I want to get better at?

Good readers set goals to challenge themselves. Your goal might be:

  • Read for 30 minutes without stopping
  • Finish a chapter and retell it in your own words
  • Learn 5 new vocabulary words per book
  • Try a new genre you've never read before
  • Read more smoothly with better expression
  • Understand what the author really means, not just the plot
